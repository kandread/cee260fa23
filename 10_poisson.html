Poisson process

  • Occurrence of events at any point in space and/or time
    • Earthquakes could strike anywhere at any time in an active region
    • Fatigue cracks can occur anywhere along a continuous weld
  • Bernoulli trials could be used but are limited
  • Poisson process (or sequence)

Example

  • Flaws occur at random along the length of a thin copper wire
  • \(X\) is the number of flaws in a length of \(L\) millimeters of wire
  • Suppose that the average number of flaws on a wire is \(\lambda\)

\[E[X]=\lambda\]

Partition the length of the wire into \(n\) intervals of small length \(\Delta L=L/n\)

  • If intervals small enough, probability that more than one flaws occurs in each is negligible
  • Flaws occur at random = same probability of flaw (\(p\))
  • Occurrence of flaw in interval independent of other intervals

Approximate with binomial \[E[X]=\lambda = n p \Rightarrow p = \dfrac{\lambda}{n}\]

\[P(X=x) \approx \binom{n}{x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}\]

\[\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty} P(X=x) = \dfrac{e^{\lambda}(\lambda)^x}{x!}\]

Proof!

A random experiment with subintervals of very small length \(\Delta L\), and

  1. Probability of more than one event in a subinterval tends to zero
  2. The probability of one event in a subinterval tends to \(\lambda\)
  3. The event in each subinterval is independent of other subintervals

is a Poisson process

Poisson distribution

The random variable \(X\) that equals the number of events in a Poisson process is a Poisson random variable with parameter \(\lambda > 0\), and \[f(x)=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda}(\lambda)^x}{x!}\]

Mean and variance

If \(X\) is a Poisson random variable with parameter \(\lambda\), then \[\mu = E[X] = \lambda\] \[\sigma^2 = Var(X) = \lambda\]
