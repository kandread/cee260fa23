Sampling from a population

Definitions

  • Population: all possible values
  • Sample: subset of the population

If \(X_1\),…, \(X_n\) are independent random variables having a common distribution \(F\), then they constitute a random sample from the distribution \(F\)

  • We can assume the distribution type (parametric inference)
  • We make minimal assumptions (non-parametric inference)

Sample statistics and distribution

A statistic is any function of the observations in a random sample.


  • sample mean, \(\overline{X}\)
  • sample variance, \(s^2\)
  • sample standard deviation, \(s\)

The probability distribution of a statistic is called a sampling distribution

Sample mean

Let's assume we have a sample from a population that has mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\)

The sample mean will be \[\overline{X} = \dfrac{X_1+X_2+...+X_n}{n}\] which is also a random variable

\[E[\overline{X}] = E \left[ \dfrac{X_1+...+X_n}{n} \right]\]

\[= \dfrac{1}{n} \left( E[X_1]+...+E[X_n] \right)\]

\[=\dfrac{1}{n} n \mu = \mu\]

\[\text{Var}(\overline{X}) = \text{Var} \left( \dfrac{X_1+...+X_n}{n} \right)\]

\[=\dfrac{1}{n^2} \left[ \text{Var}(X_1)+...+\text{Var}(X_n) \right]\]

\[=\dfrac{1}{n^2} n \sigma^2 = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\]

Central Limit Theorem

Let \(X_1\),\(X_2\),…, \(X_n\) be a set of independent and identically distributed random variables each having mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). Then for a relatively large \(n\), the distribution of \(X_1+...+X_n\) is approximately normal with mean \(n \mu\) and variance \(n \sigma^2\).

Problem 14.2

An astronomer wants to measure the distance to a star \(d\) by making a series of measurements and then using their average as an estimate. If the astronomer believes that the measured values are independent normal variables with mean of \(d\) light-years and a standard deviation of 2 light-years, how many measurements do they need to make to be at least 95% certain that their estimate is accurate to within \(\pm 0.5\) light-years?

How large a sample is needed?

  • Depends on the population distribution
  • If the distribution is normal then the sample mean \(\overline{X}\) will be normal regardless of sample size
  • General rule of thumb is that the sample size needs to be at least 30

Sample variance

Let \(X_1\),…,\(X_n\) be a random sample from a distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). If \(\overline{X}\) is the sample mean then the sample variance is \[S^2 = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^n \left( X_i - \overline{X} \right)^2}{n-1}\] and \(S=\sqrt{S^2}\) is the sample standard deviation.

Recap

Let \(X_1\),\(X_2\),…, \(X_n\) be a set of independent and identically distributed random variables each having mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). Then for a relatively large \(n\), the distribution of \(X_1+...+X_n\) is approximately normal with mean \(n \mu\) and variance \(n \sigma^2\).

For a sample \(X_1\),\(X_2\),…,\(X_n\) from a normal population with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) we have

\[\overline{X} = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i / n\] \[S^2 = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \overline{X})^2}{n-1}\]

What are their distributions?

Distribution of the sample mean

The sum of independent normal random variables is normally distributed!

\[E[\overline{X}] = \sum_{i=1}^n \dfrac{E[X_i]}{n} = \mu\]

\[\text{Var}(\overline{X}) = \dfrac{1}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\]

The sample mean \(\overline{X}\) is normally distributed with \(\text{N}(\mu,\sigma^2/n)\)

That means that \(\dfrac{\overline{X}-\mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}\) is a standard normal random variable
